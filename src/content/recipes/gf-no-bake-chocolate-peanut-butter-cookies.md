  - "3 cups old-fashioned rolled oats"
  - "1/2 cup creamy peanut butter"
  - "2 tsp vanilla extract"
  - "Pinch of salt"

  - "Medium saucepan"

## Step 1: Gather your ingredients

Have everything measured and ready before you start — once the boil begins, things move fast.

<!-- Replace with your photo: /images/recipes/no-bake-cookies/step1-ingredients.jpg -->

## Step 2: Combine sugar, milk, butter, and cocoa

Add the sugar, milk, butter, and cocoa powder to the saucepan over medium heat. Stir constantly until the butter melts and everything is combined.

<!-- Replace with your photo: /images/recipes/no-bake-cookies/step2-combine.jpg -->

## Step 3: Bring to a full rolling boil

This is the critical step. Once the mixture starts boiling, stop stirring and let it boil hard for exactly **1 minute** — set a timer. Do not skip this or the cookies won't set properly.

<!-- Replace with your photo: /images/recipes/no-bake-cookies/step3-boil.jpg -->

## Step 4: Remove from heat and add remaining ingredients

Take the pan off the heat immediately when the timer goes off. Working quickly, stir in the peanut butter until melted, then add the oats, vanilla, and salt. Mix until everything is coated.

<!-- Replace with your photo: /images/recipes/no-bake-cookies/step4-mix.jpg -->

## Step 5: Drop onto parchment and let set

Drop spoonfuls (a heaping tablespoon each) onto parchment paper. Let them sit at room temperature for 30–45 minutes until firm. Don't move them — they need to set undisturbed.

<!-- Replace with your photo: /images/recipes/no-bake-cookies/step5-drop.jpg -->

## The finished cookies

They should be firm, slightly glossy, and hold together cleanly. If they're crumbly, the boil went too long. If they're sticky and won't set, the boil was too short.

## Ok, now let's discuss

**Why do we boil something called a "no-bake" cookie?**

The name is a bit of a lie. You're not baking in the oven, but you absolutely need heat — specifically to caramelize the sugar and drive off enough moisture so the cookies can harden at room temperature.

The boil is essentially making a soft caramel. The 1-minute timer corresponds to roughly the "soft ball" stage of candy making (~235°F). At this temperature, the sugar syrup will firm up when cooled but still have enough flexibility to hold the oats together without being brittle.

**Why is the timing so precise?**

30 seconds too short → cookies never firm up, stay sticky forever.
30 seconds too long → cookies are crumbly and dry, won't hold together.

If you make these often enough to care, a candy thermometer takes the guesswork out entirely: pull at 235°F.

**Gluten-free note:** Standard rolled oats are naturally gluten-free, but are often processed on shared equipment. If you're cooking for someone with celiac disease, use certified GF oats.
